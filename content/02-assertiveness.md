---
slug: assertiveness
title: Assertiveness, boundaries & self-advocacy
default_priority: 2
core_idea: Communicate what you want clearly, without being passive or aggressive.
lessons:
  - title: Passive, aggressive, assertive
    body: |
      Three default styles. Passive: you swallow your needs to avoid friction, then
      resent it later. Aggressive: you push your needs over other people's, winning the
      moment but costing trust. Assertive: you state your view and needs directly and
      respectfully, treating the other person as an equal.

      Your style isn't fixed — most people skew passive with authority (professors,
      bosses) and more aggressive with people close to them. Step one is noticing which
      way you tilt in which context; you can't correct a lean you can't see.
  - title: The core tools
    body: |
      Assertiveness is a small set of moves you can rehearse. "I" statements own your
      experience without accusing: "I feel X when Y, and I'd like Z." Specific requests
      beat vague hints — ask for the exact thing you want. And you can say no without a
      paragraph of justification: "No, I can't take that on right now" is a complete
      sentence. Over-explaining invites negotiation you didn't want.
activities:
  - title: Style audit
    kind: audit
    est_minutes: 5
    instructions: Write 3 recent situations where you felt walked on or frustrated, and label how you responded — passive, aggressive, or assertive.
  - title: Script 3 sentences
    kind: script
    est_minutes: 5
    instructions: 'Write one "I feel… when… I''d like…" statement, one clean no ("No, I can''t do that now"), and one boundary ("I''m not available after 8 pm for work messages").'
  - title: Role-play in your head
    kind: rehearsal
    est_minutes: 5
    instructions: Take a common stress scenario (group-project scope creep, a weekend plan you don't want) and rehearse an assertive 2–3 sentence response.
  - title: Quiz — saying no
    kind: quiz
    est_minutes: 1
    prompt: What's the assertive way to decline a request you can't take on?
    choices:
      - Give a long, detailed justification so they understand
      - Say no clearly without over-explaining
      - Avoid answering and hope they forget
      - Say yes, then quietly not do it
    answer_index: 1
  - title: Quiz — the "I" statement
    kind: quiz
    est_minutes: 1
    prompt: Which is a well-formed assertive "I" statement?
    choices:
      - "You always interrupt me and it's rude."
      - "Maybe we could possibly try, if that's okay?"
      - "I feel cut off when I'm interrupted, and I'd like to finish my point."
      - "Fine, whatever you want."
    answer_index: 2
