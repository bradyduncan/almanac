title: Conflict resolution & negotiation
default_priority: 3
core_idea: Handle disagreements without drama and get better outcomes.
lessons:
  - title: Catch it early, find the interests
    body: |
      Conflict gets expensive when it's avoided or left to escalate. The skill is
      catching friction early, naming the real issue, and choosing a constructive
      process instead of letting it fester or blow up.

      Underneath most positions are interests — the why behind the what. "I need this by
      Friday" (position) might really be "I'm being judged on the launch date"
      (interest). Surface interests on both sides and you usually find options that a
      pure position-vs-position standoff would never reveal.
  - title: Separate the people from the problem
    body: |
      Attack the problem, not the person. "He's lazy" is a dead end; "he's overloaded
      and we need a clearer split" is a problem you can solve together. Reframing a
      blaming thought into a shared problem keeps the other person on your side of the
      table.

      Emotion management is central — yours and theirs. Stay calm, pause when provoked,
      and don't get dragged into power plays. The person who keeps their composure
      usually shapes the outcome.
activities:
  - title: 5-step reflection
    kind: reflection
    est_minutes: 5
    instructions: After any minor conflict, jot down what triggered it, what each side wanted, what you did, and what you'd try next time.
  - title: Reframe practice
    kind: reflection
    est_minutes: 5
    instructions: 'Take a blaming thought ("He''s lazy") and reframe it as an interest or problem ("He''s overloaded; we need a clearer task split").'
  - title: Negotiation pattern
    kind: script
    est_minutes: 5
    instructions: 'Write a mini-script that (1) states the issue, (2) states your ideal, and (3) proposes a trade ("If we ship v1 by Friday, I''ll own the demo deck").'
  - title: Quiz — positions vs interests
    kind: quiz
    est_minutes: 1
    prompt: In negotiation, an "interest" is best described as...
    choices:
      - The specific demand someone states
      - The underlying need or reason behind their demand
      - The price they first quote
      - Whoever has more power
    answer_index: 1
  - title: Quiz — handling provocation
    kind: quiz
    est_minutes: 1
    prompt: When the other person provokes you mid-conflict, the constructive move is to...
    choices:

      - Immediately concede to end it
      - Bring up every past grievance
    answer_index: 1
