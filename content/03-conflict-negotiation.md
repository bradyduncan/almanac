---
slug: conflict-negotiation
title: Conflict resolution & negotiation
default_priority: 3
core_idea: Handle disagreements without drama and get better outcomes.
levels:
  beginner:
    lessons:
      - title: Catch it early, name the issue
        body: |
          Conflict gets expensive when it's avoided or left to escalate. The cheapest
          move is catching friction early and naming the actual issue plainly, before it
          calcifies into resentment or blows up. "Hey, I think we're not on the same page
          about X — can we sort it?" defuses far more than silence does.

          Naming it isn't starting a fight; it's choosing a constructive process instead
          of letting the problem run the relationship.
      - title: Separate the people from the problem
        body: |
          Attack the problem, not the person. "You're careless" is a dead end; "these
          two bugs slipped through — how do we catch them next time?" is a problem you can
          solve together. The instant someone feels personally attacked, they defend
          instead of solve.

          Reframing a blaming thought into a shared problem keeps the other person on
          your side of the table, working the issue with you rather than against you.
    activities:
      - title: 5-step reflection
        kind: reflection
        est_minutes: 5
        instructions: After any minor conflict, jot down what triggered it, what each side wanted, what you did, and what you'd try next time.
      - title: Reframe practice
        kind: reflection
        est_minutes: 5
        instructions: 'Take a blaming thought ("He''s lazy") and reframe it as an interest or problem ("He''s overloaded; we need a clearer task split").'
      - title: Quiz — separating people from problem
        kind: quiz
        est_minutes: 1
        prompt: "\"Separate the people from the problem\" means..."
        choices:
          - Avoid the person entirely
          - Attack the issue, not the person's character
          - Win by making them feel bad
          - Always concede to keep the peace
        answer_index: 1
  intermediate:
    lessons:
      - title: Positions vs interests
        body: |
          A position is what someone demands ("I need this by Friday"); an interest is the
          why beneath it ("I'm being judged on the launch date"). Position-versus-position
          is a tug of war. Surface the interests on both sides and you usually find
          options neither of you saw — maybe a partial delivery Friday satisfies the real
          need without the full scramble.

          Ask "what's driving that for you?" and listen. Most deadlocks dissolve once the
          underlying interests are on the table.
      - title: Manage the emotion, yours first
        body: |
          Emotion, not logic, is what makes conflict spiral. When you feel the heat rise,
          slow down: a breath, a pause, "let me think about that for a second." You can't
          control whether the other person gets provoked, but staying composed keeps you
          from being dragged into a power play — and the calmer party usually shapes the
          outcome.

          If it's too hot, it's fine to table it: "I want to get this right — can we pick
          it up in an hour?" A pause is a tool, not a retreat.
    activities:
      - title: Interest excavation
        kind: reflection
        est_minutes: 5
        instructions: Take a current disagreement. Write each side's stated position, then the likely interest beneath it. Find one option that serves both interests.
      - title: Negotiation pattern
        kind: script
        est_minutes: 5
        instructions: 'Write a mini-script that (1) states the issue, (2) states your ideal, and (3) proposes a trade ("If we ship v1 by Friday, I''ll own the demo deck").'
      - title: Quiz — handling provocation
        kind: quiz
        est_minutes: 1
        prompt: When the other person provokes you mid-conflict, the constructive move is to...
        choices:
          - Match their intensity so you aren't walked over
          - Pause, stay composed, and avoid the power play
          - Immediately concede to end it
          - Bring up every past grievance
        answer_index: 1
  advanced:
    lessons:
      - title: BATNA — know your walk-away
        body: |
          Your BATNA is your Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement — what you'll do
          if you don't reach a deal. Knowing it tells you when to push and when to accept.
          If your alternative is strong (another job offer, another vendor), you can hold
          firm; if it's weak, you protect the relationship and find a workable middle.

          Before any real negotiation, decide your walk-away in advance. It stops you from
          accepting a bad deal under pressure or blowing up a good one chasing the last 5%.
      - title: Repair after rupture
        body: |
          Hard conversations sometimes leave a mark even when they end "resolved." The
          advanced skill is repair: a short, genuine follow-up that restores the
          relationship — "I pushed hard yesterday; I value working with you and want to
          make sure we're good." Repair is cheap and rare, and it compounds trust over
          years.

          Resolving the issue settles the transaction; repairing the relationship is what
          lets the next hard conversation go well.
    activities:
      - title: BATNA worksheet
        kind: reflection
        est_minutes: 5
        instructions: Pick a real upcoming negotiation. Write your best alternative if no deal happens, and the exact point at which you'd walk away.
      - title: Repair message
        kind: script
        est_minutes: 5
        instructions: Draft a 2-sentence follow-up that repairs a relationship after a tense exchange — acknowledge your part, affirm the relationship.
      - title: Quiz — BATNA
        kind: quiz
        est_minutes: 1
        prompt: Your BATNA is...
        choices:
          - The most you're willing to pay
          - Your best alternative if no agreement is reached
          - The other side's opening offer
          - A type of contract clause
        answer_index: 1
---
